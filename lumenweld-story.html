Chapter One

The Girl Who Saw Too Much

✦

Sael had always seen more than others.

Not visions, not prophecy — simply color. Where others saw the morning sky as blue, Sael saw seventeen distinct layers of it, each one a different temperature of light, each one carrying its own low harmonic she could almost hear. It made her a poor conversationalist and an extraordinary Lumenweaver's apprentice.

She was seventeen when Master Orvyn died, and the moment his Lumenweave unraveled — that delicate lattice of captured light he had spent forty years building across the rooftops of the Gilded Canopy — Sael felt it the way you feel a song stop mid-note.

The forest dimmed. Not dramatically. Just slightly. The way a room dims when someone you love leaves it.

She pressed her palm to the cold bark of the oldest tree in the Canopy and felt, faintly, the vibration of every light that had ever touched its leaves.
— The Last Lumenweaver

The Canopy Elders called it natural succession. The forest would dim, they said, and a new Lumenweaver would emerge within a generation, as had always happened.

Sael spent three days in the deep forest alone, watching the bioluminescent edges of the leaves fade from gold to a dull amber. Each night the Canopy Spirits sang a little softer. Each morning there was a little less to navigate by.

On the fourth day, she found it — coiled in the roots of the oldest tree at the forest's heart: a single thread of Master Orvyn's original weave. Still glowing. Barely. Like the last ember deciding whether to go out.

Chapter Two

The Road to Roséveil

✦

The Mirrorfen Shallows lay between the Canopy and Roséveil — three days of walking through wetlands where the sky and its reflection were indistinguishable.

Sael had traveled them once as a child, holding Master Orvyn's hand so she wouldn't step into what she thought was a gap in the ground but was actually sky. Now she walked them alone, and the disorientation was almost pleasant — a world where up and down had reached an agreement.

She found the Tidebound settlement on the second evening: a cluster of homes built precisely at the waterline, half above and half below the mirror-surface. An elder woman sat on her doorstep — her lower half reflected perfectly, her upper half dissolving into the amber dusk above.

"You carry light that doesn't belong to you," the woman said, without looking up.

Sael stopped. Around her wrist — or tucked away, or coursing through her — the thread pulsed once in response.

"It belonged to my master."

"Masters don't leave threads behind by accident." The woman finally looked at her, and her eyes were the color of still water at noon — no discernible pupil, just depth. "The question is whether you intend to finish what he started, or simply carry it until it burns you."

Sael did not answer. She sat down on the bank and watched her own reflection sit down beneath her, patient and certain in a way she was not.

— Another crossroads —

Chapter Three

Roséveil at Dusk

✦

She arrived at the gates of Roséveil at the exact moment of true dusk.

She had not planned it. But the thread on her wrist had grown warm the last hour of walking, and she had followed its warmth the way you follow a held lamp — not thinking, just going.

The city was doing the Evengiving.

Every window open. Every voice rising. Light blooming from ten thousand acts of release simultaneously — gold and rose and teal and amber — and for four minutes, Roséveil became brighter than noon, an accidental aurora generated entirely by the decision to give something beautiful away.

Sael stood at the gate and wept. She didn't know why. The thread on her wrist pulsed in perfect rhythm with the city's light.

Beauty, she understood now, was not decoration. It was the structural language the world used to tell you: you are in the right place.
— The Last Lumenweaver

In the Quartz Quarter, the oldest part of the city, she found what she had been sent to find.

A room — no, not a room. A wound in the city. A space between three ancient quartz formations where the Lumenweave was fraying at its edges, pulling apart at the seams. The light here did not glow. It flickered uncertainly, the way candlelight does in a room where someone is about to say something difficult.

Master Orvyn had known. That was why he left the thread. Not as a gift — as an instruction.

She was not here to grieve him. She was here to finish his work.

✦ Epilogue

Years later, travelers would ask the children of Roséveil about the night the Quartz Quarter blazed brighter than the rest of the city combined.

The children would smile with a particular knowing quality — the smile of people who have seen something that cannot be fully described — and they would say:

"A girl came from the Gilded Canopy carrying a thread of light. She knew what to do with it."

And that was always enough.
